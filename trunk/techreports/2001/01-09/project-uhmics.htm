Inca Software Requirements Specification

The secret life of uhmics

The person (or people) who manage the uhmics account have the following general goals:
Respond to the emails sent in a relatively timely manner
Provide approval codes to students who warrant them, and explanations and/or workarounds to those who don't.
Provide an equitable distribution of ICS course seats amongst the various "flavors" of ICS students, who register at different times during the registration season. 
Improve the caliber of students taking ICS courses

To understand how this all works, it helps to understand the meaning of "students", "courses", the "registration season", and  "uhmics emails"  from the uhmics perspective. 

Students

From the perspective of uhmics, students come in the following seven flavors, in rough order of descending priority for courses:  

  1. UH-Manoa graduating senior ICS majors are those students who are capable of graduating within the upcoming semester if they can get four or fewer ICS courses.  These are the "most favored" students for registration. Although they may not get their first choice of courses, an effort is made to get them enough courses to graduate within the upcoming semester if they so choose.  There is currently no automated means to detect these students; an ICS administrator must identify them by hand.  ICS majors get to register at the beginning of registration season. 
      
  2. UH-Manoa non-graduating undergrad ICS majors are the next most favored students with respect to registration.  These first two groups together are now large enough to fill all of our non-introductory courses.  We may in future include ICS minors in this group. As noted above, ICS majors get to register at the beginning of registration. 
      
  3. UH-Manoa undergrad non-ICS majors have relatively low priority for the more advanced courses.  An effort is made to reserve a few seats, say 10%, of upper division courses for these students. These students actually have high priority for the intro-level courses, since the higher priority students are ICS majors who typically have already taken the intro courses. Non-ICS majors typically do not get to register for ICS courses at the beginning of registration season. 
      
  4. New/Transferring undergraduates are students who are enrolling at UH-Manoa for the first time but who have taken computer science courses elsewhere, such as at the Community Colleges.  These students have two difficulties.  First, their transcripts are not available within the UH-Manoa system, so their grades and courses are not automatically available to uhmics.   New/Transferring undergraduates are barred from registration until late in the registration season.  However, there is usually a special week during registration season in which "continuing" (i.e. previously enrolled students at UH-Manoa) students are actually barred from registration and new/transferring students are allowed to register exclusively. 
      
  5. Classified ICS graduate students are students in our M.S. and Ph.D. programs.  Some of these students have undergraduate deficiencies to make up, which requires them to take upper-level undergraduate courses.  An effort is made to reserve a small percentage of seats for these students, but the general view is that ICS graduate students, even if they have deficiencies, can always find graduate level courses to take. Thus, they might be restricted to at most one undergrad course per semester. 
      
  6. Classified non-ICS graduate students are students in another graduate program at UH, such as engineering, bioengineering, chemistry, physics, or math.  These students often have a relatively reasonable computer science preparation and desire to take upper-level ICS undergrad and graduate level courses in preparation for transferring to our department as a classified graduate student. 
      
  7. Unclassified graduate students are students who have already received an undergraduate degree from some institution but who are not yet formally admitted into the ICS program.  Many of these students wish to take low-level ICS courses, such as ICS 111, 211, and 311.  However, they are almost always excluded from 111 and 211, and have only low priority for ICS 311.  These students are encouraged to take ICS 111 and 211 from a community college. 

One of the first orders of business for uhmics when reading an email is to determine as best as possible the flavor of student from the above list.   Student emails frequently incorrectly report their status, so some sort of independent verification is necessary. 

Courses

To begin, it is important to note that not all ICS courses involve uhmics.  ICS 101 is our biggest course, but also (thankfully) one whose registration is handled completely by Pa'e.    Graduate courses (i.e. ICS 500-800) with only a few exceptions do not require approval codes for graduate students. While undergraduates do need approval, their numbers are relatively small.  Finally, with a few exceptions, graduate ICS courses do not tend to fill completely.  For these reasons, uhmics has only minor involvement with graduate ICS courses. 

Uhmics also does not currently handle ICS 111 and ICS 141, basically because uhmics is already overburdened with the courses it handles.  However, there are significant advantages to placing  ICS 111 and ICS 141 under the control of the Inca system, even though uhmics does not currently handle them. First, the Inca system can provide preferential enrollment to freshman and sophomores for these courses, which Pa'e currently cannot.  Second, moving ICS 111 (in particular) to Inca means that we can generate a report on how many students indicated a desire to take ICS 111 in comparison to how many actually obtained a seat in the course. This can be helpful data to the administration for planning the future growth of our department.   

Thus, we will focus on the undergraduate courses (including ICS 111 and 141, but excluding ICS 101) . One of the most basic rules to note is that an A or B in ICS 111 is required to move on to 211, and an A or B in 211 is required to move on to any higher level course such as  ICS 212 or ICS 311.  In other words, an A or B in both ICS 111 and 211 is required for students to move on the remaining courses in our department.  This has both good and bad consequences.  The good consequence is that our advanced students tend to have a reasonable programming capability.  The bad consequence is that many students tend to repeat ICS 111 and 211 once, twice, three times or more as they attempt to get the magic B that allows them to move on.  

The registration season

The goals of uhmics are made significantly harder to achieve due to the existence of a "registration season" with various dates that impact on the various flavors of students, as well as some of our own self-imposed constraints, as well as the changeable nature of student course interests.  First, new/transferring students cannot register until late in the registration season, so some seats need to be reserved for these students.  Second, our requirement that a student have an A or B in ICS 111 in order to move on to 211 means that students taking ICS 111 in the Fall cannot be evaluated with respect to their qualifications for  ICS 211 until mid-December, which is very late in registration season.  Finally, providing a student with an approval code does not guarantee that they will actually register for the course.  

For these reasons, uhmics does not issue approval codes sufficient to fill all of the seats in all of our courses early in the season.  In addition, uhmics continues to provide additional approval codes throughout the registration season, eventually providing more approval codes for most ICS courses than the number of seats available for those courses!  This is an important feature, because some students do not use the approval code because they decide to take a different course, and other students register for the course, then drop it either prior to or early on in the semester.  The approval codes issued by uhmics are only effective for registration if there is a seat available in the class.  Thus, once uhmics has issued more approval codes for a course than there are seats available, it effectively turns over control of registration in the course to Pa'e.  

With these issues in mind, let's look at a typical registration season. This one is for Spring  2000, although the same sequence of events occur for Summer and Fall.  The following table shows the dates of various events during a registration season, and how uhmics attempts to manage the allocation of seats to courses during the various event periods.  

Date Event Implications for uhmics

Uhmics emails

To give you some idea of the workload of uhmics, in April and May of 2000 they replied to 1,468 emails regarding registration.  That's approximately 24 emails per day for two months. Review of the emails indicates that most requests tend to follow one of the following templates:

  1. Can I get approval codes for <course>?
    Response involves checking qualifications and replying with either a code, a denial of a code, a time when a code will be provided, or a request for more information. 
      
  2. I am from another institution. I would like to register in <course>.
    Response involves asking for more information in order to determine qualifications. 
      
  3. I took <course> during <semester> and got <grade>. Can I get approval codes for <course>?
    Response is similar to (1).
      
  4. I am a <grad student in another department>.  I self-studied <material>.  Can I take <course>?
    Response involves checking qualifications, then explaining protocol for classified grads in another department.
      
  5. When can I get an approval code for <course>?
    Response checks for flavor of student, then responds with date.
      
  6. I am currently taking <ICS 111 or 211>. Can I get the approval code for <ICS 211 or 311>?
    Response involves explanation of protocol for obtaining approval codes after grades are posted.
      
  7. Pa'e says there's space in <course>.  Can I get an approval code?
    Response involves explanation of protocol for when/how approval codes are provided.

The moral of this email does not concern whether uhmics acted fairly or not (it is most likely the case that uhmics did in fact act fairly).   The real moral is that the current allocation algorithm is complex enough that, even when applied fairly, students may feel that some preferential treatment might have occurred.  An important goal for the Inca system is to make the allocation algorithm visible to students, and to provide clear explanations for the decisions, so that students believe they are being treated fairly.
