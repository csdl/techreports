Inca Software Requirements Specification

Requirements in a nutshell

The 12 basic requirements

Now that we have a pretty good idea of how uhmics functions, let's start to specify the requirements for the Inca system.  The first set of requirements are taken from the current goals for the uhmics-based process, although it is hoped that the Inca system can achieve these goals more efficiently and effectively than was previously possible:

  1. Provide approval codes only to qualified students. 
    Inca enforces the prerequisites and other constraints (such as an A or B in ICS 111 or 211). Note that providing an approval code does not always guarantee a seat in the course, depending upon the time during the registration season when the code was provided. 
      
  2. Ensure that graduating ICS majors get an opportunity to enroll in courses needed to graduate. 
    This does not mean a graduating ICS major gets every course they want, but an effort should be made to ensure they can graduate. 
      
  3. Provide ICS majors with priority over non-ICS majors. 
    The Inca process will incentivize students to become and remain ICS majors. 
      
  4. Provide opportunities for lower-level students and unclassified graduates to enter into our program. 
    Inca should help ensure that seats in ICS 111 are made available to lower-level students, and that unclassified grads have an opportunity to take some undergrad courses. 
      
  5. Provide opportunities for classified grads to make up undergrad deficiencies. 
    Inca should again ensure that a small number of seats are made available for these students.
      
  6. Make the approval process equitable, visible, predictable, and understandable to students. 
    Inca should provide explanations for  its decisions and procedures so that students understand (a) how a decision is reached, (b) when a decision will be reached, (c) what their chances appear to be, and (d) what other alternatives exist for them if their desired outcome does not occur. 

The following requirements for Inca provide facilities beyond what is currently provided by the uhmics-based system:

  1. Make historical data on a student's progress through the ICS program visible to that student.
    Inca should show each student exactly what courses they've taken, their grades, and other state with respect to their ICS program that is used in the course approval process. This enables students to check to see if the data upon which decisions concerning their enrollment is accurate (and to ask an administrator to correct it if necessary and appropriate.)
     
  2. Allow students to order their request for next semester's classes.
    Many students have one course they want very to register in for a given semester, and others they are not so interested in.  The current system does not provide an opportunity for students to express these preferences.
      
  3. Provide fine-grained prioritization rules. 
    Currently, course approval is based upon "coarse-grained" prioritization, which is essentially based upon categorizing the applicant into one of the seven flavors of student. Even after applying course-grained prioritization, there may still be many more requests for a course than seats available.  For example, consider a popular course like ICS 451 with 60 seats total.  During the initial scheduled registration period for continuing students, 75% of those seats (45 total) might be available to ICS majors.  However, 60 or 70 ICS majors might request an approval code for that course. Currently, uhmics makes these decisions on a first-come, first-served basis because any other scheme would require too much work.  A potentially better strategy that is possible with Inca is to apply fine-grained rules, which would rank the set of applicants based upon their GPA, whether or not they are an ICS honors student, whether or not they have unsuccessfully attempted the course before, and so forth.  Application of fine-grained prioritization rules not only has the potential to improve the quality gaining approval to our courses, but it also has an indirect effect of incentivizing our students to work harder in their current courses, since their current grades will have a direct impact on their ability to get into future courses. 
      
  4. Provide statistical data on requests for courses. 
    The Inca system can easily generate reports on the number of requests made for specific courses, along with the number of seats actually available.  Such information on the demand for ICS courses can help the department better plan for the future. For example, with knowledge of precisely how many students actually request ICS 111 and 211, as opposed to how many successfully register, and how these numbers change from semester to semester, we can provide to the administration much better data on the need for departmental resources.
      
  5. Support notification of changes to courses and sections during registration.
     Quite frequently, new sections of courses open and others close during registration. The Inca system can be used to notify those who have requested a particular course that a new section has opened, for example.  Such late-breaking changes can also be published on the Inca website for new arrivals. 
      
  6. Above all, do no harm.
    Transition from a manual to an automated system introduces new risks: from malicious hackers to unexpected hardware and software crashes.  As course registration is mission-critical to the department, safeguards must be put in place to ensure that registration can always succeed in some form.  As an example safeguard, the Inca system should send an email message to a "log" email address (such as ics-Inca@hawaii.edu) each time a student completes a course request as well as each time the Inca system allocates an approval code. Such a mechanism provides both a secure record on a different system of the Inca actions, and preserves it in a form such that registration can be returned to manual methods quickly in the event of catastrophic failure of the system. 

The Inca-enabled registration season

Let's now take a second look at the Spring 2000 registration season as it would appear if the Inca system was in place:

Date Event Inca behavior
10/15-10/21 Inca website configuration by administrator
 During this week, the Inca administrator configures the website for use.  This involves:
Uploading the courses, sections, seating, and approval codes for the upcoming semester.
Updating the Inca student information database with data from ISIS about student courses and grades from prior semesters. 
Updating course prerequisite data, if necessary.
Specifying certain students as graduating ICS seniors, based upon ISIS data.
10/21-11/21 Inca website open to students
 For this month preceding the start of registration, students log in to the Inca system to submit or revise their course requests. During this time period, they can also check their historical data and send email to the Inca administrator with requests to fix incorrect or missing information.

The system can begin to enforce prerequisites at this point, by enabling students to request only those courses for which they are currently qualified. It can also provide explanations to students as to why they are not qualified to request a certain ICS course. 

Finally, the system can provide feedback to the students on the current number of requests that have been received for each course and the student's current "position" in line for that course, based upon their student flavor, the number of seats available, and their fine-grained prioritization.  This helps students to predict in advance whether they are likely to get an approval code for specific courses.  
11/22-11/25 Inca website closed to students for initial approval processing During these few days, the system is closed to further student requests.  This allows the Inca administrator time to review the requests, to review the preliminary course assignments made by the system, and to make any manual changes deemed necessary. Closing the website to student login during this time is helpful because it removes the possibility of last-minute student requests upsetting the prioritizations. 
11/26 Inca website reopens to students; request status emails go out.  On this date (or whenever the administrator finishes the review), the administrator instructs the system to generate an email to every student indicating the disposition of their requests and an explanation for why each request was approved, denied, or deferred.  On this first round of email notifications, the system will attempt to satisfy the following allocation strategy:
Qualified graduating senior ICS majors should get approval codes sufficient to graduate by the start date.  
Additional approval codes sufficient to fill 75% of the seats in 300 and 400-level ICS courses will be allocated to qualified non-graduating ICS majors, with preference based upon fine-grained priority.
Approval codes sufficient to fill 75% of the seats in ICS 111 and 50% of seats in ICS 211 and ICS 311 are allocated to qualified ICS majors and non-majors, with preference based upon fine-grained priority.

In most cases, students who did not get an approval code for one or more courses will be told that their request has been "deferred", and that they receive a second email during the first week of January with the final status. 

It is theoretically possible for a student to be denied a course at this point, but that would occur through Inca administrator action. The default cases are to either accept or defer any request from a (potentially) qualified student. 
11/29-12/10 Scheduled registration (continuing students only)
 Those students receiving an approval code can go ahead and register during this time. Since the number of seats exceeds the number of approval codes, everyone with an approval code at this time is guaranteed a seat in the class.  The number of seats actually taken will be smaller than the number of approvals allocated,  of course, due to students changing their minds or other factors. 

All students are also free to go back into Inca and make further course requests during this time.  These requests will be pooled and all students will be told that the decision will be emailed during the first week of January.   

The system can now provide reports for each course on: (a) the number of seats, (b) the number of approvals already allocated, (c) the number of outstanding requests, and (d) that student's position in the request line, based upon flavor, priority, etc.  There is no attempt made to integrate this information with Pa'e, so if a student is interested in the actual number of seats that remain for a course, they must check that themselves.
12/11-12/17 Unscheduled registration (continuing students only)
 No change from the previous period. 
~1/1 Last semester grades available
 Around this date, last semester grades become available.  The Inca administrator uploads this data into the system. Inca can now provide approval codes to students who got an A or B in ICS 111 or 211 from the previous semester.  Emails can be sent to students based upon the following allocation strategy:
Approval codes sufficient to fill remaining 25% of seats in ICS 111 to qualified new/transferring students. 
Approval codes sufficient to fill remaining 50% of seats in ICS 211 and ICS 311 to ICS 111 and 211 students who got an A or B this past semester.
Approval codes sufficient to fill remaining 25% of seats in 300 and 400-level courses to qualified new/transferring and unclassified grad students.

Inca can now send out request denials to those students whose grades during the prior semester are not sufficient for them to enroll in their requested courses.  For example, a student who requested ICS 211 but who received a C in ICS 111 would be sent an email denying their request for an approval code.  
1/4-1/5 Scheduled registration (new/transferring and unclassified students only)
 New/transferring and unclassified students can now register. Those with an approval are guaranteed a place in the course, since the number of approvals still does not exceed the number of seats. 
1/6-1/9 Unscheduled general registration (all students)
 On this date, the administrator can instruct Inca to do a "sweep" of its database and generate an email with an approval code for any student requesting a course for which they are qualified.  

From this point on, Inca can be configured to generate an email with an approval code immediately to any student who requests one for a course for which they are qualified. Students are no longer limited to only 4 ICS course requests; they can request as many ICS courses as they want, and they do not need to specify a priority.  

Conceptually, at this point Inca turns over control of registration to Pa'e.  Students can obtain as many approval codes as they want, but whether they can use them to get into a course depends upon whether seats are available.  
1/10-1/24 Late registration No change.
1/25 Inca website closes to students With registration over, the Inca administrator can generate reports on the demand for ICS courses, and begin making preparations for the next semester.
